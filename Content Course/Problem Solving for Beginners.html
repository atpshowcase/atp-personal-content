Fundamental Course - Hangout Style

Problem Solving Fundamentals: From Simple to Complex Problems

Learn how to think in a structured way to solve problems, not just rely on "gut feeling". Perfect for work, business, or daily life.

Quick Map of Sections:

CHAPTER A: Intro, CHAPTER B: Mindset, CHAPTER C: Identifying Problems, CHAPTER D: Root Cause Analysis, CHAPTER E: Brainstorming Solutions, CHAPTER F: Decision Making, CHAPTER G: PDCA, CHAPTER H: Design Thinking, CHAPTER I: Communication, CHAPTER J: Tools, CHAPTER K: Case Studies, CHAPTER L: Practice, CHAPTER M: Anti-Patterns, CHAPTER N: Advanced, CHAPTER O: Closing.

Sub-chapters use the format A.1, A.2, B.1, B.2, and so on.

CHAPTER A: Introduction to Problem Solving

A.1. What is Problem Solving?

Problem solving is a skill, not some magic talent you’re born with. That means anyone can learn to be a problem solver, as long as you’re willing to practice. The process is like being a detective: you gather data, analyze the situation, then look for the most reasonable solution. It’s not about guessing or just trusting your gut.

Hangout analogy: Imagine you’re chilling with friends, and suddenly your buddy’s motorbike breaks down. Someone immediately says, “Ah, must be the spark plug!” without checking anything. A true problem solver will ask first: “Did you have enough gas? Any weird noises before it died? Did you try restarting?” So, don’t just point fingers—find the root cause first.

A.2. Why is This Skill Super Important?

At work, you get paid to solve problems, not just to do tasks. People who are good at problem solving are usually the team’s go-to, trusted by the boss, and have more career opportunities. This skill is also useful outside of work, like managing your money, handling conflicts, or finding solutions when you’re broke.

  • Become the team’s MVP, not just a benchwarmer.
  • Bigger chance for promotions or moving to strategic roles.
  • Useful for managing your own life, not just office stuff.
Hangout analogy: In a futsal team, there are two types of players: those who just kick the ball as told, and those who can read the game, set up strategies, and help the team get out of trouble. The second type usually becomes the “key player” and is wanted by many teams.

A.3. Types of Problems

Not all problems can be solved the same way. There are three main types:

  1. Simple Problems: The cause is obvious, and the solution is clear.
    Example: Printer jams because paper is stuck. Just pull out the paper, done.
  2. Complicated Problems: You need special knowledge or skills to find the solution, but once you know the pattern, you can repeat it.
    Example: Car engine won’t start. You have to check one by one: battery, spark plug, fuel, etc. Usually, a mechanic knows this stuff.
  3. Complex Problems: Lots of factors are connected, and there’s no single right answer.
    Example: City traffic jams. It’s not enough to just add more roads—you have to think about public transport, driver behavior, city planning, and so on.
Hangout analogy:
- Simple problems are like instant noodles: if you run out of hot water, just boil more.
- Complicated problems are like making fancy milk coffee: you need to know the measurements, technique, tools, and recipe.
- Complex problems are like keeping your hangout group chill even when people disagree: you have to understand everyone’s personality, mood, and sometimes there’s no instant fix.

CHAPTER B: The Problem Solver's Mindset

B.1. Curiosity: That Burning Need to Know

A true problem solver is super curious, but in a good way. They’re never satisfied with surface answers, always asking “Why is it like that?”, “Is this really the only cause?”, “What if we try something else?” This curiosity keeps them from giving up and pushes them to dig until they hit the root. Sometimes, the right answer comes from the simplest questions.

Hangout analogy: Little kids who keep asking “why?” to their parents have the makings of a problem solver. They don’t just accept “because I said so.” In your hangout group, the one who asks “Why do we always chill here?” is usually the one who finds a cooler spot.

B.2. Growth Mindset: Believing You Can Level Up

Growth mindset means believing that skills can be trained, not just inherited. People with a growth mindset see problems not as walls, but as a “gym” to work out their thinking muscles. Fail? Not the end of the world—just new data to learn from. They’re not embarrassed to learn new things, even from juniors.

  • “I can’t do it yet” is better than “I can’t do it.”
  • Challenges are chances to upgrade your skills.
  • Criticism is a vitamin, not poison.
Hangout analogy: People go to the gym not because they hate their bodies, but because they believe they can get stronger. Problem solvers see problems the same way—it’s all practice to get better.

B.3. Objectivity: Don’t Take It Personally

When looking for solutions, emotions and assumptions are your enemies. Effective problem solvers always try to separate facts (data) from feelings (opinions/bias). They focus on “what’s really happening,” not “who’s to blame.” Sometimes, problems get messier because everyone gets emotional and won’t admit mistakes. Being objective is key so your solution actually fixes the problem, not just makes things feel better for a moment.

Hangout analogy: Being the referee in a futsal match between hangout groups. You have to judge the game fairly, not just because you’re closer to one team. If you’re not objective, it’ll end in a fight and no one’s happy.

B.4. Embracing Imperfection: Doesn’t Have to Be Perfect

Waiting for the perfect solution is a trap. Good problem solvers get the idea of “good enough for now.” It’s better to have an 80% solution that actually gets going, than wait for 100% perfect but never execute. Along the way, you can keep improving and upgrading the solution. What matters is real progress, not just talk.

Hangout analogy: Release version 1.0 of your app first, don’t wait for every feature before launching. User feedback helps you make version 1.1, 1.2, and so on. In your hangout, it’s better to just meet up even if the place isn’t perfect, than wait for everyone to agree and never actually hang out.

CHAPTER C: The Art of Identifying Problems

C.1. Problem vs. Symptom

This is a classic trap that happens all the time—at work, in business, even in daily life. Lots of people get busy dealing with symptoms, but never touch the root cause. Symptoms are just surface signs, like an alarm going off. The real problem is usually deeper and sometimes not obvious at first. If you only treat the symptoms, the problem will come back, maybe even worse.

  • Symptom: "I have a headache." (what you see/feel)
  • The Real Problem: Lack of sleep, dehydration, or maybe high blood pressure. (the root cause)

Hangout tip: If your motorbike makes a weird noise, don’t just replace the exhaust. The problem could be in the engine, oil, or even bad gas. Don’t jump to conclusions just from what you see.

Hangout analogy: A wet floor is a symptom. The real problem might be a leaky roof. If you just mop the floor without fixing the roof, it’ll keep getting wet. Same with relationships—if you just apologize without fixing your attitude, the problem will come back.

C.2. The "Observe and Ask" Technique

The most basic (and often most powerful) way to identify problems: observe directly and ask. Don’t just rely on reports, Excel data, or third-party stories. Go to the “field,” see the process yourself, and talk to people who actually experience the problem. Sometimes the best insights come from casual chats, not formal meetings.

  • Observe: See the process yourself, don’t just trust reports.
  • Ask: Talk to people directly involved, not just the boss.

Hangout tip: If a friend suddenly gets quiet, don’t just assume they’re mad. Try chatting casually—maybe they have problems at home or work.

Hangout analogy: A good chef doesn’t just read recipes, but also tastes the food directly. They also ask customers, “How does it taste? Anything missing?” In your hangout, sometimes solutions pop up while chilling over coffee, not during official meetings.

C.3. Problem Statement: Defining the Problem Clearly

The problem statement is the main foundation before you start looking for solutions. One sentence that’s clear, concise, and not open to multiple interpretations, so everyone on the team understands the same problem and doesn’t go off track. A good problem statement usually includes: context (background), impact (why it matters), and the gap between the current state and the ideal.

  • Context: What’s happening?
  • Impact: What’s the bad effect if it’s not solved?
  • Gap: What’s the difference between now and the ideal target?

Example template:
“Currently [what’s happening], the impact is [loss/problem caused]. Ideally, we want to reach [the desired condition].”

Hangout tip: If you want to hold an event, make sure everyone understands the main problem. For example: “Right now our hangout is quiet because the place isn’t comfy. As a result, fewer people come. Ideally, we want a cozier place so it’s lively again.”

Hangout analogy: The problem statement is like GPS coordinates for the team. Without it, everyone goes in the direction they think is right, but never reaches the same destination. In your hangout, without a clear goal, you just end up going in circles.

CHAPTER D: Root Cause Analysis (RCA)

D.1. What is Root Cause Analysis?

Root Cause Analysis (RCA) is a systematic process to find the root cause of a problem, not just deal with surface effects. The goal is simple: if you pull out the root, the problem tree won’t grow back. Many people just focus on symptoms, but if you don’t fix the root, the problem will return, maybe even worse. RCA teaches us not to be satisfied with instant fixes, but to really dig until you find the main source.

  • Main RCA steps: Identify the problem, dig for causes, check data/facts, and make sure the solution really targets the root, not just the surface.

Hangout tip: If someone in your group is always late, don’t just scold them every time. Find out why they’re late—maybe they live far away, transport is tough, or they have family stuff. Then find the right solution.

Hangout analogy: If there are weeds in the garden, you can cut the top (treating the symptom), or pull them out by the roots (RCA). The first will grow back, the second is done for good. Same with life problems—if you just cover up the effects, they’ll come back.

D.2. The 5 Whys Technique

5 Whys is a super simple but powerful technique to dig for the root cause. Here’s how: you ask “Why?” to every answer you get, usually up to five times (or until you find the root). Each answer to “Why?” becomes the basis for the next question. This technique forces us not to be satisfied with surface answers, and to keep digging until we find the real source.

  • Case example: Website is down.
  • 1. Why? Server error.
  • 2. Why? Server memory is full.
  • 3. Why? There’s a process eating up memory abnormally.
  • 4. Why? There’s a bug in the new image upload feature.
  • 5. Why? The testing process didn’t cover large file scenarios. (Root Cause)

Hangout tip: If your friend is always sleepy when hanging out, don’t just say they need more sleep. Keep asking: Why not enough sleep? Turns out, too much work. Why too much work? Turns out, they can’t say no to their boss. And so on, until you find the root cause.

Hangout analogy: 5 Whys is like peeling an onion. You take off each layer until you find the core. Sometimes it makes you cry, but it’s worth it!

D.3. Fishbone Diagram (Ishikawa)

The Fishbone Diagram (or Ishikawa Diagram) is a visual tool to help us map out all possible causes of a problem in a structured way. Usually, it’s divided into main categories, like: Man (people), Machine (tools), Method (work process), Material (materials), Measurement (measurement), Environment (environment). Each category can be broken down into specific causes.

Short history: The Fishbone Diagram was first introduced by Kaoru Ishikawa, a quality expert from Japan, around the 1960s. It was first used in manufacturing for quality analysis, but now it’s used everywhere—from work, business, to hangouts for daily problems. That’s why it’s sometimes called the "Ishikawa Diagram" to credit the inventor.

  • Fish head: Problem Statement (the main problem to solve)
  • Main bones: Cause categories (Man, Machine, etc.)
  • Spines: Specific causes in each category

Hangout tip: If your group often fights over small things, try making a simple fishbone diagram. Break down the problem: who’s involved, how’s the venue, are the rules clear, is the vibe right, etc. Usually, you’ll spot the root of the fight.

Hangout analogy: Fishbone diagram is like a mind map just for finding causes. You draw out all possibilities so nothing gets missed. In your hangout, it’s like listing all the reasons why your friend is hard to invite out, so the solution isn’t just a wild guess.

CHAPTER E: Brainstorming & Generating Solutions

E.1. The Importance of Quantity First

At the start of brainstorming, don’t rush to judge which ideas are good or bad. Main focus: quantity first, quality later. Why? The more ideas you get out, the bigger the chance you’ll find an out-of-the-box solution. Sometimes the weirdest idea sparks the coolest solution. So, write down everything—crazy ideas, impossible ideas, silly ideas, just put them all in. Don’t hold back or be shy. Later, after all the ideas are out, then you pick and filter.

  • Hangout tip: When brainstorming with friends, keep the vibe chill. Don’t judge or laugh at anyone’s idea. That way, everyone’s brave enough to speak up.
Hangout analogy: Brainstorming is like fishing with a big net. Haul up all the fish, big or small. Pick which ones to cook later on land, not while you’re still at sea. So, don’t throw away fish (ideas) while you’re still at sea!

E.2. Mind Mapping Technique

Mind mapping is a visual technique that makes brainstorming more fun and structured. Here’s how: write the main problem in the center of a paper or board, then draw lines out for each idea or solution category. From each main branch, you can draw sub-branches for more ideas. With a mind map, your brain is free to think in all directions, not stuck in a linear order. Often, connections between ideas that you didn’t see before pop up because of the mind map.

  • Hangout tip: Use colored markers or sticky notes to make it easier to see connections between ideas. If you’re in a group, let everyone add their own branches.
Hangout analogy: Mind mapping is like making a treasure map for ideas. You draw all possible paths, crossroads, and hidden spots. Sometimes, the treasure is found on a path you never expected.

E.3. SCAMPER Technique

SCAMPER is like a secret move to “force” your brain to think creatively from existing solutions. SCAMPER stands for:

  • Substitute: What can you replace? (e.g., swap materials, people, or processes)
  • Combine: What can you combine? (Combine two features, two ideas, or two teams)
  • Adapt: What can you adapt from elsewhere? (e.g., adapt a strategy from another industry)
  • Modify: What can you change? (Size, shape, color, how it works)
  • Put to another use: What else can it be used for? (New function for an old thing/idea)
  • Eliminate: What can you remove or simplify? (To make it more efficient)
  • Reverse/Rearrange: What can you reverse the order of? (Process, steps, or structure)

With SCAMPER, you don’t just wait for inspiration—you actively “mess around” with existing ideas to get something new. Perfect for teams that often get stuck with old ideas.

Hangout analogy: SCAMPER is like playing with LEGO. You have the blocks, but you try to build something new by mixing and matching in different ways. Sometimes the result is unexpected, but that’s what makes it fun!

CHAPTER F: Decision Making for Solutions

F.1. From Tons of Ideas to the Best Choice

After brainstorming, you usually end up with a mountain of ideas. Now, your main job is to filter and pick which ideas are most worth executing. This selection process needs a cool head, objectivity, and no hard feelings. Sometimes your own idea has to be sacrificed for a more realistic solution. Focus on the most feasible ideas, those with big impact, and that fit your resources. Don’t forget to involve the team so the judgment is fair and not biased.

  • Hangout tip: Hold an open discussion session, everyone can give pro-con arguments for each idea. Don’t get upset if your idea isn’t picked.
Hangout analogy: After fishing with a big net, now it’s time to pick which fish to cook, which to sell, and which to release. Not all fish have to be cooked—same with ideas, not all have to be executed.

F.2. Pro/Cons Analysis Technique

Pro/Cons Analysis is the simplest but most effective idea selection technique. Here’s how: for each solution idea, write down the pros and cons. This way, you’re forced to think about both sides of every idea, not just the good stuff. Sometimes, an idea that looks cool turns out to have a fatal flaw you only see after writing it down.

  • Hangout tip: Discuss together, so you get more perspectives. Sometimes your friend sees a con you didn’t notice.
Hangout analogy: Like buying a motorbike. You list the pluses (fuel efficient, fast) and minuses (expensive, hard to find spare parts). Then weigh them before deciding to buy or not.

F.3. Decision Matrix (Eisenhower Matrix)

The Decision Matrix (sometimes called the Eisenhower Matrix) is a more structured and objective idea selection technique. Steps:

  1. First, set the evaluation criteria (e.g., Cost, Implementation Time, Impact, Risk, Ease of Execution).
  2. Give each criterion a weight based on priority (e.g., Impact 40%, Cost 20%, etc.).
  3. Each solution idea is rated one by one based on those criteria, then multiplied by the weight.
  4. Add up all the scores, the idea with the highest score wins.

This technique is super useful for making objective, accountable decisions, especially if your team is big or there are lots of stakeholders. The whole process is transparent, so there’s no drama like “my idea didn’t get picked because the boss didn’t like it.”

  • Hangout tip: Make the matrix on a whiteboard or spreadsheet, so everyone can see and help score.
Hangout analogy: Decision matrix is like holding an audition. All contestants (ideas) are judged by the same jury (criteria) and clear scores. No “jury’s favorite” nonsense. So, the result is fair and everyone can accept it.

CHAPTER G: PDCA Cycle: Plan, Do, Check, Act

G.1. What is the PDCA Cycle?

PDCA (Plan-Do-Check-Act) is a classic framework for continuous improvement. This framework isn’t just for factories or big companies—it’s also great for managing projects, team work, or even daily stuff. The point is, PDCA teaches us not to be satisfied with a one-time result—there’s always room for evaluation and improvement. The cycle keeps spinning: plan, do, check the results, then fix and repeat. With PDCA, your solutions are more measured, not random, and you can be accountable for them.

  • Short history: The PDCA concept was first popularized by Dr. W. Edwards Deming, a management expert from America, inspired by Walter Shewhart’s ideas. It was first used for quality control in Japanese manufacturing, but now it’s a global standard for improvement in all fields.
Hangout analogy: PDCA is like a chef’s recipe. Plan the menu (Plan), cook (Do), taste it (Check), then adjust the seasoning if needed (Act). Tomorrow, use the improved recipe again. So, every time you cook, you get better!

G.2. Plan: Planning the Solution

In the Plan stage, you really need to nail down your plan before starting execution. Start by setting a clear goal, making a hypothesis (initial guess), and laying out detailed steps to run the solution. Don’t forget to set success indicators, who’s responsible, and what risks might pop up. The more detailed your plan, the less likely you’ll fail halfway.

  • Hangout tip: Write the plan on a whiteboard or Google Doc, so everyone on the team can see and give input. Don’t let anyone “just join in” without understanding the plan.
Hangout analogy: Before a road trip, you make a plan first. Where to go, which route, how much gas you need, where to stay. Don’t just hit the gas, or you’ll get lost.

G.3. Do: Executing the Plan

In the Do stage, all the plans you made get put into action. If possible, start small or with a pilot project, so if something goes wrong you don’t lose big. Here, it’s super important to record all processes, results, and obstacles you find. Data from this stage becomes material for evaluation in the next step. Don’t forget, execution should follow the plan—don’t suddenly improvise without a clear reason.

  • Hangout tip: Document the process, even if it’s just with photos, notes, or voice memos. Makes it easier to check what’s been done later.
Hangout analogy: After planning the road trip, now it’s time to start the car and go. Record all expenses and events on the road, so it’s easy to evaluate later.

G.4. Check: Checking the Results

In the Check stage, you compare the real results from execution (Do) with the targets set in the plan (Plan). Did the results meet expectations? Any gaps or deviations? All data and notes from the previous stage should be analyzed to find out what worked, what failed, and why. This stage is super important so you don’t just move on to the next step without knowing the real situation.

  • Hangout tip: Get the whole team together for a review. Sometimes, someone else’s perspective can spot problems you missed.
Hangout analogy: In the middle of the trip, you check the map and the car’s condition. Still on track? Enough gas? Any unexpected problems? Don’t wait until you’re really lost to realize it.

G.5. Act: Taking Action

In the Act stage, you make decisions based on the evaluation (Check). If the solution worked, standardize and apply it on a bigger scale. If something’s missing or failed, find out why, then plan improvements for the next cycle. The point is, don’t stop at one cycle—keep repeating PDCA until the results are really optimal. This cycle is what makes improvement never-ending.

  • Hangout tip: Don’t be afraid to admit mistakes and start over. Usually, this is where the best improvements come from.
Hangout analogy: If the road trip goes smoothly, you can keep going. If there’s a problem (like a flat tire), you take action (change the tire), then replan the rest of the trip (Act/Plan again). The journey doesn’t stop just because of one problem.

CHAPTER H: Introduction to Design Thinking

H.1. What is Design Thinking?

Design Thinking is a problem-solving approach that’s all about people (human-centered). The goal is to find solutions that aren’t just technically correct, but also truly needed and loved by users. The process is iterative and non-linear.

Analogy: Making a chair. The engineer thinks about how to make the chair strong enough to hold weight. The designer thinks about how to make the chair comfy and nice to look at. Design Thinking combines both.

H.2. Empathize: Understanding the User

This is the most crucial stage. You really have to understand your users: what their problems are, what they need, what makes them happy or frustrated. You can do this through interviews, observation, or experiencing things from their perspective.

Analogy: Before making a new menu, a chef talks to customers to find out their tastes. They don’t make food they like, but what the customers like.

H.3. Define: Defining the Problem

After gathering insights from the empathy stage, you formulate a sharp problem statement from the user’s point of view. The focus is on the user’s “needs,” not the product’s “features.”

Analogy: From chatting with customers, the chef concludes: “Customers need breakfast that’s fast, healthy, and filling.” Not “Customers need a sandwich with A, B, C.”

H.4. Ideate: Creating Solution Ideas

This is the brainstorming phase. All the techniques from CHAPTER E can be used here. The goal is to come up with as many solution ideas as possible to answer the user’s needs you’ve defined.

Analogy: The chef and their team start brainstorming ideas: oatmeal, smoothies, energy bars, healthy rice dishes, etc.

H.5. Prototype & Test

Make a simple version of your idea (prototype), then test it directly with users. The prototype doesn’t have to be perfect—it can be just a sketch, mock-up, or rough model. The goal is to get quick feedback at low cost. From the test results, you can go back to the previous stage to make improvements.

Analogy: The chef makes a few sample menu items in small portions (prototype), then gives them to customers to taste (test). Whichever is liked most, that’s what gets developed further.

CHAPTER I: Communication for Problem Solvers

I.1. Presenting the Problem and Solution

Finding a solution is only half the journey. The other half is selling that solution to stakeholders (team, boss, client). Bad communication can make a genius solution get rejected instantly. The key: present the problem and solution as a story that’s easy to understand.

Analogy: A scientist discovers a great medicine but can’t explain how it works to doctors and patients—the discovery is wasted. Presentation is the bridge between idea and implementation.

I.2. Storytelling with Data

Data without a story is just boring numbers. On the other hand, a story without data is just empty talk. Combine both. Use data to strengthen your argument, and wrap it in an engaging narrative. Start from the problem, the discovery process, to the proposed solution and its impact.

Analogy: In detective movies, the climax is when the detective explains all the clues (data) and weaves them into a logical story to reveal the culprit.

I.3. Gaining Buy-In from Stakeholders

Buy-in means getting approval and support from important people. How? First, understand what matters to them. Present the solution from their benefit point of view. If talking to the boss, focus on business impact (saving costs, increasing revenue). If talking to the tech team, focus on work efficiency.

Analogy: Selling a car. To dads, you highlight the fuel efficiency. To young people, you highlight the design and speed. Same product, different sales pitch.

CHAPTER J: Tools for Problem Solving

J.1. Digital Whiteboards (Miro, FigJam)

These tools are like unlimited digital whiteboards. Perfect for brainstorming, making mind maps, or drawing fishbone diagrams collaboratively. Especially for remote teams, these are a must-have.

Analogy: It’s a super whiteboard with ink that never runs out and you can access it from anywhere.

J.2. Project Management Tools (Trello, Asana, Jira)

Once a solution is agreed on, execution needs to be managed. These tools help break work into small tasks, assign responsibility, and track progress. So it’s clear who’s doing what and how far along things are.

Analogy: The job board at a workshop. You can see which car is being worked on, who the mechanic is, and what needs to be replaced.

J.3. Note-Taking Apps (Notion, Obsidian, OneNote)

The problem-solving process gathers a lot of information. To keep it from getting scattered, use note-taking apps. You can store research results, meeting notes, even draft solution ideas. Notion is great for collaboration, Obsidian is awesome for personal knowledge management.

Analogy: A detective’s notebook. All clues, photos, and interrogation results are kept in one place so they’re easy to find again.

CHAPTER K: Case Studies: Applying The Frameworks

K.1. Case Study 1: "Why Did Online Store Sales Drop?"

Problem: Sales in the online store dropped 30% in the last month.

  • Identification: The symptom is a drop in sales. We need to find out if this is a real problem or just seasonal fluctuation. (Check data from the same month last year).
  • RCA (5 Whys): Why did it drop? -> Traffic dropped. Why? -> Ads were stopped. Why? -> Marketing budget was cut. Why? -> Company is saving costs. Why? -> Profitability is down. (The root cause is actually a bigger company finance issue).
  • Brainstorming (SCAMPER): How to increase sales without an ad budget? (S) Switch marketing channel to organic (SEO, content). (C) Collaborate on promos with other brands. (E) Remove products that don’t sell.
  • Decision Making: Chose to focus on SEO because of its long-term impact (using Decision Matrix).
  • Execution (PDCA): (P) Plan to optimize the 3 most popular blog articles. (D) Do the optimization. (C) Check ranking and traffic after 2 weeks. (A) If successful, apply to 10 more articles.
Lesson: A problem that looks like it’s in one department (sales) might have its root in another (finance). Don’t think too narrowly.

K.2. Case Study 2: "New Employee Onboarding is a Mess"

Problem: New employees are often confused in their first week, ask the same questions, and take a long time to become productive.

  • Design Thinking - Empathize: Interviewed several new employees. Findings: they don’t know who to ask, documents are scattered everywhere, and there’s no clear checklist.
  • Define: "New employees need a single centralized source of information and a step-by-step guide in their first week so they feel confident and quickly independent."
  • Ideate: Make an onboarding portal in Notion, create a buddy system, make tutorial videos, create a structured orientation schedule.
  • Prototype: Make a simple Notion page with a first-day checklist and important contacts.
  • Test: Try it out with the next 2 new employees. Ask for their feedback. Turns out they prefer having a “buddy” (mentor) they can ask directly.
  • Iteration: Combine the Notion portal with the buddy program.
Lesson: Involve the “user” (new employee) in the process. The best solutions come from directly understanding their struggles.

CHAPTER L: Practice & Exercises

L.1. Exercise 1: The "Everyday Problem"

Pick a small everyday problem. Example: “Room is always messy,” “Always wake up late,” “Spending too much on lunch.”

Apply these steps:

  1. Write a clear problem statement.
  2. Use 5 Whys to find the root cause.
  3. Brainstorm at least 5 solution ideas.
  4. Pick one solution using Pro/Cons analysis.
  5. Make a simple execution plan (PDCA).
The goal of this exercise is to train your brain to think structurally, even for small problems.

L.2. Exercise 2: The "Observation Challenge"

Go to a public place (cafe, station, supermarket). Observe for 15 minutes. Try to identify at least 3 “problems” or “inefficiencies” happening. Example: “Long cashier line even though one cashier is free,” “People confused looking for a certain product,” “Door design is hard to open when carrying stuff.”

For each problem, write down a hypothesis for the cause.

The goal of this exercise is to train your “observation muscle” and sensitivity to problems around you.

L.3. Exercise 3: The "Group Challenge"

Invite 2-3 friends. Pick a case study (from the news, or a common problem in your area). Do a brainstorming session using a digital whiteboard. Everyone must contribute ideas without criticizing others’ ideas at first.

After 15 minutes, group similar ideas, then vote to pick the top 3 ideas.

The goal of this exercise is to practice collaboration, holding back from judging ideas, and selecting ideas democratically.

CHAPTER M: Common Anti-Patterns in Problem Solving

M.1. Analysis Paralysis: Too Much Thinking, No Action

This is when you keep analyzing the problem, gathering data, and weighing options until you never actually make a decision or take any action. Afraid of making a mistake, you end up doing nothing.

Analogy: You want to cross the street, but you count the number of cars, wind speed, even the chance of a meteor falling. In the end, you’re still on the curb until night.

M.2. Jumping to Solutions: Skipping Straight to the Fix

The opposite of analysis paralysis. This happens when you jump to conclusions and pick a solution before really understanding the problem. Usually because you rely on assumptions or past experience that might not be relevant.

Analogy: A doctor who immediately prescribes cough medicine as soon as a patient arrives, even though the patient hasn’t said they also have shortness of breath and a fever. The diagnosis is shallow.

M.3. Confirmation Bias: Only Looking for Validation

Confirmation bias is our tendency to look for, interpret, and remember information that supports our existing beliefs. We become blind to data or perspectives that contradict us, even if they’re actually right.

Analogy: You’re a fan of a certain phone brand. When researching, you only read good reviews about that phone and ignore all the bad ones.

M.4. "Not Invented Here" Syndrome

This is when a team or individual rejects a good idea or solution just because it came from outside. There’s a reluctance to adopt external solutions because “our own solution must be better,” even if that’s not true.

Analogy: Refusing to use Google Maps and insisting on making your own map from scratch. The result: you get lost and waste time.

CHAPTER N: Advanced Topics

N.1. Systems Thinking: Seeing the Big Picture

Systems thinking is seeing problems not as isolated events, but as part of a bigger, interconnected system. Changing one part can affect other parts in unexpected ways.

Analogy: Fixing a traffic jam on one street by closing that street (local solution) actually causes worse jams on all the surrounding streets (systemic impact). Systems thinking considers all the effects.

N.2. Probabilistic Thinking: Thinking in Probabilities

The world isn’t certain. Probabilistic thinking means recognizing that nothing is 100% right or wrong, but there are “chances” or “probabilities.” This helps us make decisions in uncertainty more rationally.

Analogy: The weather forecast says “70% chance of rain.” You don’t know for sure if it’ll rain, but you decide to bring an umbrella. That’s probabilistic thinking.

N.3. First Principles Thinking: Breaking It Down to the Basics

This is a way of thinking by breaking a problem down to its most basic elements (first principles), then building a solution from there. It’s the opposite of “reasoning by analogy” (just following what others do). Innovators like Elon Musk use this technique a lot.

Analogy: Others make better suitcases by adding wheels or zippers (analogy). Elon Musk thinks: “What is a suitcase? A thing to carry stuff. What’s the most efficient way to move stuff?” That’s how the Hyperloop idea came up.

CHAPTER O: Conclusion: Becoming a Lifelong Problem Solver

O.1. Problem Solving is a Journey, Not a Destination

Congrats! You’ve reached the end of the material. But this isn’t the finish line. Becoming a great problem solver is a lifelong journey. The frameworks and tools you’ve learned here are your map and compass, but you have to walk the journey yourself.

Analogy: Learning a recipe is easy. Becoming a great chef takes thousands of hours in the kitchen, trying different ingredients, and being brave enough to fail.

O.2. The Power of Deliberate Practice

Don’t just wait for problems to come. Practice this skill deliberately. Do the exercises in CHAPTER L regularly. Try applying these frameworks to work problems, even personal ones. The more you practice, the more natural this skill feels.

Analogy: Pro athletes don’t just train before a match. They train every day, repeating the basics thousands of times until perfect.

O.3. Final Words: Go Find Some Problems!

The world is full of problems waiting to be solved. Don’t run from problems—chase them. Every problem is a chance for you to grow, learn, and make an impact. Now you’ve got the tools. Use them well.

Final Conclusion: Problem solving isn’t about being the smartest person, but about being the most willing to learn and the most persistent in finding a way out. It’s a mix of curiosity, humility to admit you don’t know, and the courage to try.
